## How to Use These Templates

Templates are starting points, not scripts. Every email must be personalized with at least 3 signals from CRM data or meeting transcripts. The formulas below show the structure -- fill them with specific details from the 5-layer data model.

**Template selection rule:** Match the template to the follow-up type detected in Layer 5 of the SKILL.md instructions. If the follow-up spans two types (e.g., post-demo + proposal sent), blend the templates but prioritize the more recent activity.

## Post-Discovery Follow-up

**When to use:** After the first substantive conversation where you learned about their situation, pain points, and goals. Typically 1-3 days after a discovery call.

**Goal:** Prove you listened, share something useful, and advance to the next step.

### Subject Line Formula
`[Specific topic discussed] + [next step hint]`

Examples:
- "Reducing your QA backlog -- next steps"
- "Pipeline visibility for the Austin team"
- "Cutting onboarding from 6 weeks to 2"

### Opening Sentence Formula
`Reference a specific pain point or statement they made + acknowledge its impact.`

Examples:
- "You mentioned your team spends 12 hours a week on manual pipeline reporting -- that's a significant drain on selling time."
- "The challenge you described with onboarding new reps in Austin resonated -- losing 6 weeks of productivity per hire adds up fast."

### Value-add Examples
- A relevant article, report, or benchmark related to their pain
- A brief comparison of how a similar company solved the same problem
- An insight or data point they didn't have ("Companies in your space typically see X")
- A framework or checklist related to their challenge

### CTA Examples
- "Would a 30-minute demo focused specifically on pipeline automation make sense as a next step?"
- "I've blocked Thursday 2pm -- does that work for a deeper dive on the QA workflow?"
- "Could you loop in [person they mentioned] for the technical review?"

### Annotated GOOD Example

> **Subject:** Cutting your QA backlog -- resource + next step
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> Great conversation yesterday. You mentioned your team reviews 200+ PRs monthly and the backlog keeps growing despite adding headcount -- that's a pattern we've seen across engineering orgs scaling past 50 devs.
>
> I pulled together a short case study from Relay (similar team size, similar stack) showing how they cut review cycles by 40% without adding tooling complexity. Thought it might be useful as you evaluate options.
>
> Would it make sense to schedule a 30-minute walkthrough focused specifically on your PR review workflow? I have Thursday at 2pm open.
>
> Best,
> Alex

**Why this works:**
- Opens with a specific detail from the conversation (200+ PRs, headcount)
- Value-add is relevant and useful (case study from similar company)
- CTA is specific (30 min, focused topic, proposed time)
- 118 words -- concise but substantive

### Annotated BAD Example

> **Subject:** Great meeting!
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> Thanks for taking the time to chat with me yesterday. It was great learning about your company and the challenges you're facing. I think our platform could really help streamline your processes and improve efficiency.
>
> I'd love to schedule a follow-up call to dive deeper into how we can help. Would sometime next week work?
>
> Let me know your thoughts!
>
> Best,
> Alex

**Why this fails:**
- Subject line is generic (no context)
- Opening is boilerplate ("thanks for taking the time")
- No specific detail from the conversation referenced
- "Streamline processes and improve efficiency" is empty jargon
- No value-add provided
- CTA is vague ("sometime next week" + "let me know your thoughts")
- Zero personalization signals

## Post-Demo Follow-up

**When to use:** After showing the product or service in action. Typically within 4 hours of the demo while impressions are fresh.

**Goal:** Reinforce the moments that resonated, address questions raised, and propose the evaluation next step.

### Subject Line Formula
`[Feature/moment they reacted to] + [evaluation hint]`

Examples:
- "The dashboard view you asked about + trial details"
- "Automating your weekly reports -- evaluation plan"
- "Custom fields for your workflow -- next steps"

### Opening Sentence Formula
`Reference the specific feature or moment where they showed excitement, asked a question, or leaned in.`

Examples:
- "I noticed the automated report builder got the most questions from your team -- that tells me reporting is the pain point worth solving first."
- "When you saw the real-time sync with Salesforce, you mentioned it would save your ops team 5 hours a week -- let's make sure that estimate holds up."

### Value-add Examples
- Answers to questions asked during the demo that you didn't fully address
- A comparison grid showing how features map to their specific requirements
- A recorded clip of the specific feature they cared about most
- Implementation timeline tailored to their use case

### CTA Examples
- "Shall I set up a sandbox with your team's actual data for a hands-on evaluation?"
- "Would it help to schedule a technical deep-dive with your engineering lead?"
- "I've drafted a proposed evaluation plan -- can you review and flag any gaps?"

### Annotated GOOD Example

> **Subject:** Real-time Salesforce sync -- trial setup
>
> Hi Marcus,
>
> During the demo, your team zeroed in on the real-time Salesforce sync -- especially the bi-directional field mapping. You asked whether custom objects sync automatically, and I want to confirm: yes, custom objects are supported out of the box with our Enterprise plan.
>
> I've attached a comparison grid mapping your 6 requirements from the RFP to our capabilities. The one gap is the Marketo integration, which we're shipping in Q2 -- I can connect you with our PM for a timeline if that's a blocker.
>
> Would a 2-week sandbox with your actual Salesforce data be useful as a next step? I can have it ready by Friday.
>
> Best,
> Jordan

**Why this works:**
- Opens with the specific moment that resonated (Salesforce sync, bi-directional mapping)
- Answers a question from the demo (custom objects)
- Value-add is highly specific (comparison grid against their RFP)
- Honestly flags a gap (Marketo) with a mitigation plan
- CTA is concrete (2-week sandbox, ready by Friday)

### Annotated BAD Example

> **Subject:** Following up on our demo
>
> Hi Marcus,
>
> Thank you for attending the demo today. I hope you found it informative and that it gave you a good overview of our platform's capabilities.
>
> As discussed, our solution offers robust integration capabilities, advanced analytics, and a user-friendly interface that your team will love.
>
> Please let me know if you have any questions or if you'd like to move forward with next steps.
>
> Best regards,
> Jordan

**Why this fails:**
- Subject line is generic
- "Hope you found it informative" adds nothing
- Repeats generic feature categories instead of specific moments from the demo
- "Robust integration capabilities" and "user-friendly interface" are meaningless phrases
- No value-add (didn't answer any questions or provide resources)
- CTA is passive ("let me know if you have any questions")
- Could be sent after any demo to any company

## Post-Proposal Follow-up

**When to use:** 3+ business days after sending a proposal with no response. The silence is the signal -- they're evaluating, comparing, or stuck on something.

**Goal:** Address the likely sticking point proactively, add context that supports the decision, and create gentle urgency.

### Subject Line Formula
`[Specific proposal element] + [helpful angle]`

Examples:
- "Implementation timeline for your Q2 launch"
- "ROI breakdown for the 3-team rollout"
- "Answers to common questions on the Enterprise plan"

### Opening Sentence Formula
`Reference a specific section of the proposal + anticipate their likely question about it.`

Examples:
- "I wanted to add context to the implementation timeline in the proposal -- the 6-week estimate assumes your IT team can allocate 10 hours/week, which you mentioned might be tight."
- "I realized the ROI section might understate the impact for your team -- I ran the numbers again using your actual pipeline volume and the savings are closer to $180K annually."

### Value-add Examples
- Updated ROI calculation using their specific data
- Answers to the 3 most common questions about that proposal type
- A reference customer willing to take a call
- An implementation checklist showing exactly what the first 30 days look like

### CTA Examples
- "Would a 15-minute call to walk through the implementation section be helpful?"
- "Can I connect you with [reference customer] who went through a similar rollout?"
- "Are there specific sections where you need more detail before moving to legal review?"

### Annotated GOOD Example

> **Subject:** ROI update -- $180K annual savings for your team
>
> Hi Priya,
>
> After our conversation about pipeline volume, I realized the ROI model in the proposal was conservative. I re-ran the numbers using your actual data (850 deals/quarter, 4 reps) and the projected savings come to $180K annually -- $45K more than the original estimate.
>
> I've attached the updated model. The biggest driver is the 3.2 hours/rep/week saved on manual CRM entry, which compounds significantly at your deal volume.
>
> Are there specific sections of the proposal you'd like to discuss before it goes to procurement? Happy to schedule a 15-minute walkthrough.
>
> Best,
> Sam

**Why this works:**
- Opens with new, specific value (updated ROI using their data)
- References exact numbers from their business (850 deals, 4 reps)
- Value-add is tangible (updated model attached)
- CTA acknowledges their likely next step (procurement) and offers to help

### Annotated BAD Example

> **Subject:** Checking in on the proposal
>
> Hi Priya,
>
> I wanted to follow up on the proposal I sent last week. Have you had a chance to review it? I'm confident our solution is the right fit for your team and I'd love to discuss any questions you might have.
>
> We're running a promotion this quarter that could save you 15% if we can finalize by month-end.
>
> Let me know when you're available to chat.
>
> Best,
> Sam

**Why this fails:**
- "Checking in" is dead language
- "Have you had a chance to review it" puts them on the defensive
- No new information or value provided
- Fake urgency (quarter-end promotion) erodes trust
- "Let me know when you're available" is passive

## Post-Negotiation Follow-up

**When to use:** After a negotiation session where terms were discussed, concessions were made, or agreements were reached. Typically within 24 hours.

**Goal:** Confirm what was agreed, document outstanding items, and set the path to signature.

### Subject Line Formula
`[Key agreement] + [next milestone]`

Examples:
- "Confirmed: 3-year terms + Q2 start -- contract draft"
- "Agreed pricing for 50-seat rollout -- redline timeline"
- "Implementation scope confirmed -- next: legal review"

### Opening Sentence Formula
`Summarize the key agreement reached + confirm mutual understanding.`

Examples:
- "Great progress today. To confirm: we've aligned on the 3-year Enterprise plan at $X/seat with the implementation support package included."
- "Thanks for working through the details today. I want to make sure we're aligned on the three items we agreed to."

### Value-add Examples
- A clean summary of all agreed terms (so they don't have to reconstruct from memory)
- Answers to concerns raised during negotiation
- An implementation timeline showing what happens after signature
- A mutual action plan with dates and owners

### CTA Examples
- "I'll have the contract draft to you by Wednesday. Can you confirm who on your side handles the legal review?"
- "Are there any items I missed in the summary below?"
- "What's the best way to coordinate the security review -- should I loop in your IT team directly?"

### Annotated GOOD Example

> **Subject:** Agreed terms + contract draft by Wednesday
>
> Hi David,
>
> Thank you for working through the details today. Here's my understanding of what we agreed:
>
> 1. 50-seat Enterprise license at $X/seat/year (3-year term)
> 2. Implementation support included (dedicated CSM for first 90 days)
> 3. SSO and API access included at no additional cost
> 4. Payment terms: net-45
>
> **Still open:** Security questionnaire (I'll send ours by EOD tomorrow) and the data residency requirement (confirming EU hosting availability with our infrastructure team).
>
> I'll have the contract draft in your inbox by Wednesday. Who on your team should I copy for the legal review?
>
> Best,
> Jamie

**Why this works:**
- Immediately confirms all agreed terms (reduces misunderstanding risk)
- Clearly separates "agreed" from "still open"
- Commits to specific next steps with dates (contract by Wednesday, security questionnaire by EOD)
- CTA advances the process (identifying legal reviewer)

### Annotated BAD Example

> **Subject:** Next steps
>
> Hi David,
>
> Great discussion today! I think we made a lot of progress. I'll get the paperwork started on our end and circle back with you soon.
>
> Looking forward to working together!
>
> Best,
> Jamie

**Why this fails:**
- Subject line is generic
- No specific terms confirmed (invites misunderstanding)
- "Circle back with you soon" has no commitment or timeline
- Does not surface open items
- No CTA
- The recipient has no record of what was agreed

## Re-engagement Follow-up

**When to use:** When a contact or deal has gone quiet -- 7+ days since last response, or 14+ days since meaningful engagement. The relationship is cooling and needs a new reason to respond.

**Goal:** Provide a new trigger or value that gives them a reason to re-engage without pressure.

### Subject Line Formula
`[New trigger/value] + [low-pressure framing]`

Examples:
- "New benchmark data for SaaS onboarding teams"
- "Thought of your team when I saw this"
- "Quick update on the integration you asked about"

### Opening Sentence Formula
`Lead with the new value or trigger -- NOT with acknowledgment of the gap. Never reference the silence directly.`

Examples:
- "I came across a benchmark report on SaaS onboarding metrics that maps directly to the challenge you described with rep ramp time."
- "Quick update: the Marketo integration you asked about in our last call shipped last week."

### Value-add Examples
- New content (report, article, benchmark) relevant to their stated pain
- Product update that addresses a concern they raised
- A customer success story from their industry published since your last contact
- A relevant industry event or trend that affects their business
- Company news of theirs that you can genuinely comment on

### CTA Examples
- "Worth a quick look? Happy to walk through the implications for your team."
- "If this is still on your radar, I'd suggest a 15-minute check-in to see what's changed."
- "No pressure either way -- just wanted to make sure you had this."

### Annotated GOOD Example

> **Subject:** New SaaS onboarding benchmarks -- your team vs. industry
>
> Hi Rachel,
>
> I came across Bessemer's 2025 SaaS onboarding report and immediately thought of your team. The benchmark for time-to-first-value in your segment is 18 days -- you mentioned yours was closer to 45. That gap represents significant revenue at risk.
>
> The report breaks down what the top quartile does differently. I highlighted the sections most relevant to your stack on page 12 and 17.
>
> If onboarding is still a priority this quarter, I'd love to spend 15 minutes mapping their framework to your situation. If timing has shifted, no pressure at all.
>
> Best,
> Taylor

**Why this works:**
- Leads with genuine value (relevant benchmark report)
- References specific detail from past conversation (45-day time-to-value)
- Value-add is specific and useful (highlighted relevant pages)
- CTA is low-pressure with an explicit out ("if timing has shifted")
- Never mentions the gap in communication

### Annotated BAD Example

> **Subject:** Touching base
>
> Hi Rachel,
>
> I haven't heard back from you in a while and wanted to check in. I know things get busy, but I wanted to make sure our proposal didn't fall through the cracks.
>
> Is this still a priority for your team? I'd hate for you to miss out on the savings we discussed.
>
> Please let me know either way so I can update my records.
>
> Best,
> Taylor

**Why this fails:**
- "Touching base" is dead language
- "I haven't heard back" is guilt-tripping
- "Didn't fall through the cracks" is condescending
- "I'd hate for you to miss out" is manipulative urgency
- "Update my records" reveals self-interest, not value
- Zero value provided -- no reason for them to respond

## Deliverable Handoff

**When to use:** When completing a task, sending a resource, or delivering something the contact requested. The follow-up is the delivery itself.

**Goal:** Deliver the item, explain how to use it, and set the next step.

### Subject Line Formula
`[What was delivered] + [what to do with it]`

Examples:
- "ROI model -- customized for your team's metrics"
- "Technical architecture doc -- ready for your review"
- "Competitive comparison -- as discussed Thursday"

### Opening Sentence Formula
`Deliver immediately -- no preamble.`

Examples:
- "Attached is the ROI model customized with your team's metrics from our Thursday call."
- "Here's the technical architecture document covering the SSO and API integration requirements you outlined."

### Value-add Examples
- A short guide on how to read or use the deliverable
- Key takeaways or highlights (save them from reading the whole thing)
- Additional context that makes the deliverable more useful

### CTA Examples
- "Would a 15-minute walkthrough of the model be helpful, or is the doc self-explanatory?"
- "Can you share this with your IT team and let me know if they have questions by Friday?"
- "I'd recommend reviewing Section 3 first -- that's where your team's requirements are mapped."

### Annotated GOOD Example

> **Subject:** Custom ROI model -- your team's actual numbers
>
> Hi Lin,
>
> Attached is the ROI model built from the metrics you shared Thursday:
>
> - 12 reps, $85K average deal size, 90-day cycle
> - Projected savings: $156K/year (primarily from reduced admin time)
> - Break-even at 4.5 months
>
> Key assumptions are in the yellow cells -- feel free to adjust if your numbers have shifted. The sensitivity analysis on tab 2 shows impact at different adoption rates.
>
> Would it be useful to walk through this together before you share it with finance? I have Tuesday 10am open.
>
> Best,
> Morgan

**Why this works:**
- Delivers immediately (no throat-clearing)
- Summarizes key takeaways (they don't need to open the file to get the headline)
- Empowers them to adjust (yellow cells, sensitivity analysis)
- CTA is specific and anticipates their next step (sharing with finance)

### Annotated BAD Example

> **Subject:** Deliverable attached
>
> Hi Lin,
>
> As promised, please find attached the ROI model we discussed. Let me know if you have any questions.
>
> Best,
> Morgan

**Why this fails:**
- Subject line is generic
- "As promised, please find attached" is corporate filler
- No summary of what's in the deliverable or why it matters
- No guidance on how to use it
- "Let me know if you have any questions" is a dead-end CTA

## Cross-Template Rules

These rules apply across all templates:

1. **The 3-signal rule**: Every email must contain at least 3 personalization signals (specific details from CRM, transcripts, or enrichment). If you can't find 3, your data gathering was insufficient -- go back to the layers.

2. **The "so what" test**: Every sentence must pass the "so what?" test. If the recipient could respond "so what?" to a sentence, cut it or make it specific.

3. **The swap test**: If you could swap the recipient's name for any other contact and the email still works, it's not personalized enough. Rewrite.

4. **Word count discipline**: 80-150 words for the body. Under 80 lacks substance. Over 150 won't get read. Executive emails should be under 80.

5. **One CTA only**: Multiple asks create decision paralysis. Pick the single most important next step and make that the CTA.

6. **Subject line test**: Would you open this email based on the subject line alone? If not, rewrite it.

7. **The "printed and pinned" test**: If someone printed this email and pinned it to their wall as an example of great sales communication, would it hold up? If not, it's not ready.
