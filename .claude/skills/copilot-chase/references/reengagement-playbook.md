# Re-Engagement Playbook

This reference document provides the strategic framework behind the Chase skill. It covers the psychology of prospect silence, multi-channel chase sequences, when to stop, multi-threading, trigger event monitoring, and the graceful exit strategy.

---

## The Psychology of Silence: Why Prospects Go Quiet

Understanding why a prospect stopped responding is the foundation of an effective re-engagement strategy. Silence is not a single phenomenon -- it has distinct causes, each requiring a different approach. Misdiagnosing the cause leads to the wrong response.

### The 9 Reasons Prospects Go Silent

#### 1. Genuine Busy (35-40% of Cases)

The most common reason and the most overestimated. The prospect intends to respond but has not found the time. Competing priorities consumed their attention. This is especially common during Q4 (budget season), Q1 (new year planning), and around major company milestones.

**Signal:** They were engaged and positive in the last interaction. No negative signals. The gap is 5-14 days.

**Response:** Light value-add. Give them a reason to respond that is not about you. Share something useful and make the ask small.

#### 2. Internal Reprioritization (15-20%)

The initiative you discussed was deprioritized. It may come back, but not this quarter. A new executive mandate, budget reallocation, or organizational restructuring has shifted focus elsewhere.

**Signal:** Silence coincides with known internal changes (leadership transition, layoffs, restructuring). The deal was early-stage.

**Response:** Acknowledge directly. "Has the priority shifted?" gives them permission to be honest. Plant a seed for re-engagement when timing is better.

#### 3. Sticker Shock (10-15%)

The prospect saw pricing (formally or via research) and decided it was outside their budget. They are avoiding the awkward "too expensive" conversation.

**Signal:** Silence immediately follows a pricing discussion or proposal delivery. The deal was mid-stage.

**Response:** Reframe value, not price. Share ROI data, cost-of-inaction analysis, or flexible pricing options. Do not lead with a discount -- it devalues everything discussed so far.

#### 4. Chose a Competitor (10-12%)

They went with someone else and do not want the uncomfortable conversation. Some prospects ghost rather than reject.

**Signal:** Silence after a competitive evaluation. The prospect mentioned specific competitors. The competitor recently announced a new customer win in the same industry.

**Response:** The breakup email. Give them the graceful out. Some will respond honestly, creating future opportunity. Never burn the bridge -- 15-20% of "lost" deals reopen within 18 months.

#### 5. Champion Left or Changed Roles (5-8%)

Your internal champion changed jobs, got promoted into a different function, or left the company. The initiative lost its internal sponsor.

**Signal:** LinkedIn shows a role change. Company news mentions restructuring. A new contact appears in your deal.

**Response:** Multi-thread immediately. Reach out to the new person in the role. Also follow up with the departed champion at their new company -- they may buy again.

#### 6. Consensus Failure (5-8%)

Your champion tried to get internal buy-in and failed. They could not convince their boss, the procurement team pushed back, or IT raised security concerns. The champion is embarrassed and avoiding you.

**Signal:** Silence after they said "I need to run this by [stakeholder]." The deal was mid-to-late stage. The champion was enthusiastic but not the economic buyer.

**Response:** Offer to help. "I have materials that might help make the internal case" is more useful than "What happened?" Provide an ROI calculator, security whitepaper, or executive summary they can forward.

#### 7. Decision Fatigue (3-5%)

The prospect is overwhelmed by options. They are evaluating too many vendors, drowning in features lists, and have entered analysis paralysis. The easier path is to do nothing.

**Signal:** They mentioned evaluating multiple options. Extended evaluation timeline. Asking for increasingly detailed comparisons.

**Response:** Simplify. "Based on what you told me about [their top 2 priorities], here is what I would recommend and why." Cut through the noise with a clear recommendation.

#### 8. Bad Timing (3-5%)

The need is real but the timing is wrong. Budget is not available until next quarter. A prerequisite project must finish first. They are waiting for board approval.

**Signal:** They mentioned specific timing constraints in the last conversation. The deal stage has not changed.

**Response:** Acknowledge the timing and set a specific follow-up date. "You mentioned the board meets in March. I will check back the week of March 10 -- does that work?" Concrete is better than "sometime next quarter."

#### 9. You Dropped the Ball (2-3%)

You missed a commitment, were slow to respond, delivered a subpar demo, or said something that damaged trust. The prospect is not ghosting the product -- they are ghosting you.

**Signal:** Review the last interaction carefully. Did you promise to send something and forget? Was there a miscommunication? Did the demo go off-script?

**Response:** Own it directly. "I realize I owe you [the thing you promised]. I apologize for the delay." Accountability rebuilds trust faster than deflection.

## Silence Duration Analysis Framework

How long a prospect has been silent is a strong signal of what is happening and what to do about it. This framework provides benchmarks based on analysis of B2B sales cycles.

### 1-4 Days: Normal Response Window

**What it means:** Nothing. This is normal business cadence. Most B2B professionals take 1-3 business days to respond to non-urgent emails.

**What to do:** Nothing. Do not chase. Wait until day 5 at minimum. Sending a follow-up before day 5 signals insecurity and impatience.

**Exception:** If they committed to a specific action by a specific date ("I will send you the requirements by Thursday") and missed it, a brief check-in on the next business day is appropriate.

### 5-7 Days: Cooling Period

**What it means:** They are likely busy (Reason 1). The silence is probably not about you. At this stage, 60-70% of prospects will eventually respond without any follow-up.

**Risk level:** Low.

**Action:** Send a single light-touch value-add. No urgency, no pressure. If they were going to respond, this nudge accelerates it. If they were not, it keeps you top of mind without being annoying.

### 8-14 Days: Attention Drift

**What it means:** You have likely fallen off their mental priority list. The initiative may still be alive, but you are no longer in active consideration. This is the most critical window -- after 14 days, win probability drops by 30% (Gong data).

**Risk level:** Medium.

**Action:** Pattern break. Do not repeat the same approach. New angle, new channel, new value proposition. The goal is to interrupt the drift and create a new reason to engage.

### 15-21 Days: Active Disengagement

**What it means:** Something has changed. This is no longer "busy." The prospect has actively deprioritized, chosen an alternative, or lost internal support. The silence is a message.

**Risk level:** High.

**Action:** Direct check-in. Drop the value-add pretense and ask the honest question: "Has the priority shifted?" This respects their time and your own. Continued value-add emails at this stage feel tone-deaf.

### 22-30 Days: Decision Made

**What it means:** In most B2B cycles, 30 days of silence after active engagement means a decision has been made -- and it was not in your favor. The prospect may not have communicated it, but the silence IS the communication.

**Risk level:** Critical.

**Action:** The breakup email. Permission to close. This is counterintuitively the highest-response-rate format because it removes all pressure and triggers loss aversion. Data shows breakup emails get 2-3x the response rate of standard follow-ups (Yesware, 2023 analysis of 2.1M emails).

### 30+ Days: Cold

**What it means:** The original conversation is effectively dead. Do not treat it as a warm lead. Any re-engagement must be triggered by a genuine external event, not by your desire to chase.

**Risk level:** Dormant.

**Action:** Stop chasing. Move to trigger-event monitoring. Only re-engage when you have a legitimate new reason: company news, leadership change, industry development, or their initiative appearing in public forums. Without a trigger event, execute the graceful exit.

## Multi-Channel Chase Sequences

Email alone is not a re-engagement strategy. A multi-channel approach increases response rates by 3-5x compared to single-channel follow-up (Outreach.io benchmark data).

### The Standard 4-Channel Sequence

This is the default sequence when you do not have strong channel preference data from the prospect.

**Day 0 -- Email:** Value-add message matching the silence duration tier. This establishes your reason for re-engaging and provides a written reference they can return to.

**Day 3 -- LinkedIn:** A short, casual message that references the email without repeating it. "Sent you a note about [topic] -- figured LinkedIn might be easier to catch you." This channel works because it feels less formal and more personal.

**Day 5 -- Phone call:** A 30-second voicemail if they do not answer (they usually will not). The voicemail references the email and LinkedIn message, creating a pattern of touchpoints without repetition. "Not trying to stalk you -- just wanted to flag [one specific thing]."

**Day 7 -- Text message:** Only if you have an existing text relationship. Ultra-short, ultra-casual. "Hey [First Name] -- did my email about [topic] land? No rush." If you do NOT have a text relationship, skip this channel entirely.

**Day 10 -- Assessment:** If no response across all 4 channels, escalate to the next silence-duration tier or execute the multi-threading strategy.

### Channel-Priority Sequence (Adaptive)

When you have engagement history data, adapt the sequence.

**If they historically respond to email:** Lead with email on day 0. Use LinkedIn as secondary on day 4. Phone as tertiary on day 7.

**If they historically respond to LinkedIn:** Lead with LinkedIn on day 0. Follow with email on day 2 (so they have a written reference). Phone on day 5.

**If they historically respond to phone:** Call first on day 0. Send a follow-up email within 1 hour summarizing what you wanted to discuss. LinkedIn connection request on day 3 if not already connected.

**If they historically respond to text:** Text first on day 0. Follow with email on day 1. This only applies to prospects with an established text relationship.

### Channel Fatigue Rules

- **Same channel, max 3 touches.** Never send more than 3 emails, 3 LinkedIn messages, or leave 3 voicemails without a response. After 3 touches on any channel, that channel is exhausted.
- **Total touches across all channels: max 8-10 over 30 days.** More than this is harassment, not persistence.
- **Minimum 3 days between touches on the same channel.** Daily emails from a salesperson destroy relationships.
- **Never duplicate content across channels.** Each touch must add something new -- a different angle, new information, or a different ask.

## When to Stop Chasing: The Diminishing Returns Curve

Knowing when to stop is as important as knowing how to start. Sales persistence has a clear point of diminishing returns, and pushing past it damages both the relationship and your reputation.

### The Data

Analysis of 4.5M B2B outreach sequences (Salesloft, 2023) reveals:

- **Touch 1:** 8-12% response rate
- **Touch 2:** 10-14% response rate (highest -- the first follow-up is often the most effective)
- **Touch 3:** 6-9% response rate
- **Touch 4:** 4-6% response rate
- **Touch 5:** 2-4% response rate
- **Touch 6+:** Less than 2% incremental response rate
- **Touch 8+:** Negative returns -- each additional touch increases unsubscribe and block rates

### The Stop Rules

1. **After 3 emails with no response:** Switch channels. Do not send a 4th email.
2. **After 5 total touches with no response:** Escalate to breakup/permission-to-close regardless of silence duration.
3. **After the breakup email with no response:** Stop. Move to trigger-event monitoring. The prospect has communicated their answer through silence.
4. **If they respond "not interested" or "bad timing":** Stop immediately. Add to the re-engagement watch list with the reason and a 90-day check-back.
5. **If they respond with any engagement (even "maybe later"):** Reset the sequence. This is a warm signal. Acknowledge, confirm timing, and set a specific follow-up date.

### The Cost of Over-Chasing

- **Brand damage:** Prospects talk to each other. Being known as "the company that will not stop emailing" is a market-level problem.
- **Spam risk:** Repeated unreplied emails increase your sender domain's spam score. This affects deliverability for ALL your outreach, not just this prospect.
- **Opportunity cost:** Time spent chasing dead leads is time not spent on responsive prospects. The math is clear: a 2% response rate on touch 8 means 98% of your time is wasted.

## Multi-Threading: When the Primary Contact Is Unresponsive

Multi-threading is reaching out to other stakeholders at the same company when your primary contact goes dark. It is one of the most effective re-engagement strategies, but it must be executed carefully to avoid political damage.

### When to Multi-Thread

- Primary contact has been unresponsive for 14+ days despite multi-channel outreach
- The deal involves multiple stakeholders (you are not single-threaded by design)
- You have identified other contacts at the account through CRM, LinkedIn, or meeting attendees
- The deal value justifies the effort and risk

### When NOT to Multi-Thread

- The primary contact explicitly said "not now" or "not interested" -- respect the answer
- You have no legitimate business reason to contact the other person
- The primary contact is a gatekeeper who would view outreach to their boss as going over their head
- The deal is low-value and does not justify the complexity

### Multi-Threading Execution

#### Step 1: Identify the Right Second Contact

Priority order:
1. **Someone who was in a previous meeting** -- you have a legitimate connection
2. **A peer of your primary contact** -- same level, parallel function
3. **A stakeholder mentioned in transcripts** -- "Sarah mentioned your VP of Ops is involved"
4. **An executive sponsor** -- only if the deal is significant and stalled at a high level

#### Step 2: Craft the Outreach

**Critical rule:** Never say or imply that you are going around your primary contact. The message to the second contact must have its own standalone value.

**Template:**
> Hi [Second Contact],
>
> I have been working with [Primary Contact] on [topic/initiative]. I came across [relevant insight/resource] that I think is relevant to [second contact's function/role].
>
> [1-2 sentences of genuine value for THIS person, not a rehash of the deal.]
>
> Would it be helpful to include you in our next conversation about this?

#### Step 3: Inform (Do Not Blindside) the Primary Contact

If the primary contact eventually re-engages, be transparent: "I also connected with [Second Contact] about [topic] -- wanted to make sure the right people are in the loop." This preserves trust.

## Trigger Event Monitoring

After active chasing is exhausted, the most effective re-engagement strategy is trigger-event monitoring: watching for external events that create a legitimate new reason to reach out.

### High-Value Trigger Events

#### Company-Level Events
- **Funding round announced:** New capital means new initiatives and new budgets. Re-engage within 48 hours of the announcement.
- **Leadership change:** New CXO often means new strategy and new vendor evaluations. Wait 2-3 weeks for them to settle, then reach out.
- **Acquisition or merger:** Integration creates new challenges and new buying opportunities. Wait 30 days post-announcement.
- **Product launch:** They are investing in growth. Connect your solution to their expansion.
- **Layoffs or restructuring:** Proceed carefully. This can create urgency ("do more with less") but the tone must be sensitive.
- **Earnings report (public companies):** Positive earnings create budget confidence. Negative earnings create cost-cutting urgency. Both are conversation starters.

#### Contact-Level Events
- **Role change or promotion:** New role often means new buying authority and new priorities.
- **Company change:** Your champion at a new company is one of the warmest leads possible.
- **LinkedIn post or article:** Engaging with their content is the most natural re-engagement on the platform.
- **Speaking engagement or podcast appearance:** Reference something specific they said. This stands out.
- **Work anniversary:** Low-value as a standalone trigger, but a natural touchpoint combined with a substantive message.

#### Industry-Level Events
- **Regulatory change:** New compliance requirements create urgent buying needs.
- **Competitor move:** A competitor's acquisition, product launch, or pricing change creates context for your conversation.
- **Market report or benchmark:** Share relevant data that connects to their stated challenges.

### How to Monitor

1. **Google Alerts:** Set up alerts for the prospect's company name, key contacts, and industry terms
2. **LinkedIn notifications:** Follow the company and key contacts. Enable post notifications for champions.
3. **CRM reminders:** Set 90-day check-back reminders for dormant deals with notes on what trigger events to watch for
4. **Industry newsletters:** Subscribe to the prospect's industry publications for market-level triggers
5. **Job board monitoring:** If the prospect's company posts jobs in your solution's domain, it signals active investment in that area

## The Graceful Exit Strategy

The graceful exit is not giving up -- it is the strategic decision to stop active pursuit while keeping the door open. Data consistently shows that 15-20% of graceful exits result in re-engagement within 12-18 months (HubSpot, longitudinal analysis of 850K closed-lost deals, 2022).

### Why It Works

1. **Removes pressure:** Prospects who feel pursued avoid you. Removing the pursuit makes you safe to re-engage with when the time is right.
2. **Preserves reputation:** You are remembered as professional and respectful, not desperate.
3. **Creates loss aversion:** "They stopped pursuing me" triggers subtle loss aversion. The option is no longer guaranteed.
4. **Frees your capacity:** You redirect energy to responsive prospects while the passive pipeline matures.

### Executing the Graceful Exit

#### Step 1: The Final Message

Send the breakup email (Tier 4 template). This is the formal close of active pursuit.

#### Step 2: CRM Documentation

Update the deal record:
- Stage: "Closed - Nurture" (not "Closed - Lost" unless they explicitly said no)
- Notes: Summary of all interactions, the reason for silence (your best assessment), and what trigger events to monitor
- Next action date: 90 days from today
- Tags: "graceful-exit", "trigger-monitoring"

#### Step 3: Passive Monitoring

Move to trigger-event monitoring (see above). No outbound contact unless a genuine trigger event occurs.

#### Step 4: The 90-Day Check-Back

At the 90-day mark, evaluate:
- Has a trigger event occurred? If yes, re-engage with a trigger-event template.
- Has the contact changed roles or companies? If yes, reach out at the new company.
- Has the company's situation changed materially? If yes, adapt and re-engage.
- None of the above? Reset the timer for another 90 days. After 3 cycles (270 days) with no trigger event, move to "Closed - Lost."

### The Re-Engagement Window

When a graceful exit prospect does re-engage (either they reach back out or a trigger event creates an opening), the first touchpoint is critical:

- **Do NOT reference the previous silence.** They know. You know. Bringing it up creates awkwardness.
- **Do NOT restart from scratch.** Reference the previous conversation context to show you remember and value the relationship.
- **DO lead with the trigger event or their outreach.** Make the conversation about the new context, not the old gap.
- **DO be flexible on timing.** They came back on their schedule. Meet them where they are.

## Seasonal and Cyclical Patterns in Responsiveness

Response rates are not constant. They follow predictable patterns that should inform your chase timing.

### Weekly Patterns
- **Monday:** Low response rates. People are processing weekend backlog and planning the week.
- **Tuesday-Thursday:** Peak response rates. Tuesday morning and Thursday afternoon are optimal windows.
- **Friday:** Declining response rates after 2pm. People are mentally entering the weekend.
- **Weekend:** Near-zero for B2B. Do not send weekend emails -- it signals poor boundaries.

### Monthly Patterns
- **First week of the month:** Moderate. Planning and goal-setting absorb attention.
- **Second and third weeks:** Peak responsiveness. Work cadence is established.
- **Last week of the month:** Variable. End-of-month pressure can drive urgency (good for closing) or consume bandwidth (bad for net-new conversations).

### Quarterly Patterns
- **First month of quarter (Jan, Apr, Jul, Oct):** Good for re-engagement. New quarter energy and new budgets.
- **Mid-quarter (Feb, May, Aug, Nov):** Peak responsiveness. Execution mode.
- **End of quarter (Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec):** Difficult for net-new conversations. Everyone is closing existing deals and projects. Use this time for trigger-event monitoring, not active chasing.

### Annual Patterns
- **January:** High re-engagement potential. New year, new budgets, fresh start mindset.
- **February-March:** Good execution months. Chase actively.
- **April:** New fiscal year for many companies. Budget approvals happening.
- **May-June:** Strong months. Q2 execution mode.
- **July-August:** Summer slowdown in many markets. Response rates drop 15-25%. Adjust expectations.
- **September:** "Back to school" for business. High re-engagement potential after summer lull.
- **October-November:** Budget planning season. Good for positioning for next year.
- **December:** Dead zone for new conversations after the 15th. Use for planning and prep, not chasing.

### How to Apply Seasonal Data

1. **Do not chase into known dead zones.** A December 20th chase email is wasted.
2. **Time re-engagement to seasonal peaks.** If a deal went dormant in November, the January "new year, new budget" window is your best trigger.
3. **Acknowledge the season in your messaging.** "I know Q4 close is consuming everything" shows awareness. Generic timing-ignorant messages signal automation.
4. **Adjust silence-duration thresholds seasonally.** 14 days of silence in February is different from 14 days of silence in late December. Apply a seasonal multiplier: extend patience by 50% during known slow periods (July-August, late December).
