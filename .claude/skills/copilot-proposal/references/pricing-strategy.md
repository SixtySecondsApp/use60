## The 3-Tier Anchoring Framework

The most effective proposal pricing uses three tiers. Not two. Not four. Three. This is backed by research on choice architecture (Goldilocks Effect) and anchoring bias.

### The Three Tiers

| Tier | Name | Purpose | Pricing Relative to Target |
|------|------|---------|---------------------------|
| **Tier 3** (shown first) | Scale / Premium / Enterprise | Anchor high. Sets the reference point. | 1.5-2x target price |
| **Tier 2** (highlighted) | Growth / Professional / Standard | The one you want them to buy. | Target price |
| **Tier 1** (shown last) | Starter / Essential / Foundation | Safety net. Gets them in the door. | 0.5-0.7x target price |

### Design Rules

1. **Present highest first.** The first number the prospect sees becomes the anchor. Everything after feels cheaper by comparison.

2. **Highlight the middle tier.** Use visual emphasis (bold, "Most Popular," "Recommended"). 60-70% of buyers choose the middle option when presented this way (Behavioral Economics research, Simonson 1992).

3. **Make the middle tier the obvious choice.** It should include 80% of the value for 60% of the top-tier price. The gap between Tier 1 and Tier 2 should feel small. The gap between Tier 2 and Tier 3 should feel large.

4. **Differentiate on outcomes, not features.** "10 users" vs "25 users" is a feature. "Cover your sales team" vs "cover your entire GTM org" is an outcome.

5. **Name tiers for their buyer.** "Starter" for small teams testing the waters. "Growth" for teams scaling. "Scale/Enterprise" for companies with complex needs. Never use "Basic" -- nobody wants to buy "basic."

### Example Tier Structure

```
SCALE — $4,800/month
For GTM organizations with 50+ reps
- Everything in Growth, plus:
- Dedicated success manager
- Custom integrations (Salesforce, HubSpot, custom CRM)
- Advanced analytics and forecasting
- Priority support (4-hour SLA)
- Quarterly business reviews

GROWTH (Recommended) — $2,400/month
For teams of 10-50 reps ready to scale
- Everything in Starter, plus:
- AI proposal generation
- Meeting intelligence and coaching
- Pipeline health scoring
- Team dashboards and reporting
- Email support (24-hour SLA)

STARTER — $800/month
For small teams getting started
- Up to 10 users
- CRM integration
- Meeting scheduling
- Basic task management
- Community support
```

### Tier Math

A proven formula for tier spacing:

- **Starter**: Base price (P)
- **Growth**: 2.5x to 3.5x base price -- includes core differentiating features
- **Scale**: 5x to 7x base price -- includes everything plus premium services

The ratio matters more than the absolute numbers. If Growth feels like a natural step up from Starter, and Scale feels aspirational but justified, the architecture is working.

## Pricing Psychology Principles

### 1. Anchoring

The first number presented becomes the reference point for all subsequent numbers. This is the single most powerful pricing principle.

**Application in proposals:**
- Always show the highest tier first
- If you mention industry benchmarks, cite a higher one before your price
- If the prospect mentioned a budget range, acknowledge the range before presenting your price below the top end

**Example:** "Companies in your space typically invest $80K-$120K for a solution like this. Our Growth tier at $58K gives you the core capabilities at a lower entry point."

### 2. The Decoy Effect (Asymmetric Dominance)

When a third option is added that is clearly inferior to one option but not the other, it pushes buyers toward the "dominant" option.

**Application in proposals:**
- Design the Starter tier to be clearly limited in a way that matters to THIS prospect
- If they need 15 users, make Starter cap at 10. Growth at 25 feels like the natural choice.
- The Starter tier is not meant to be bought. It is meant to make Growth look like the right decision.

### 3. Price Framing

How you present the number changes its perceived magnitude.

| Framing | Example | When to Use |
|---------|---------|------------|
| Annual total | "$28,800/year" | When the number is competitive and you want one decision |
| Monthly breakdown | "$2,400/month" | When the annual number feels large |
| Per-user | "$96/user/month" | When per-user cost is competitive |
| Daily cost | "$80/day" | When selling to budget-conscious buyers ("less than your team's daily coffee budget") |
| Cost vs. cost-of-problem | "$28.8K vs. $180K lost" | When you have solid ROI data from discovery |

**Rule:** Use the framing that makes the price feel smallest relative to the value. If ROI data is strong, always frame as investment-vs-cost.

### 4. Odd/Charm Pricing vs. Round Pricing

| Price Type | Example | Signals | When to Use |
|-----------|---------|---------|------------|
| Charm pricing | $2,397/mo | Value-oriented, competitive | SMB, price-sensitive buyers |
| Round pricing | $2,400/mo | Premium, confident, simple | Mid-market, enterprise, professional services |

**Rule for proposals:** Use round pricing. Proposals are not e-commerce product pages. Charm pricing in a B2B proposal looks cheap, not smart.

### 5. Loss Aversion

People feel losses 2x more strongly than equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky). Frame the cost of NOT buying as a loss, not the cost of buying as an investment.

**Application:** In the ROI section, always include "Cost of Inaction" -- what they lose every month they do not have your solution. "Every month without automated proposals costs your team ~$15K in rep time. The Growth tier pays for itself in 5 days."

## Discount Guidelines

Discounts are powerful but dangerous. Every discount you offer sets the expectation for the next negotiation. Use them strategically, never reactively.

### When to Discount

| Scenario | Discount Range | Condition |
|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Competitive bake-off** | 10-15% | Must be tied to a decision deadline ("this pricing is valid if you decide by {date}") |
| **Volume commitment** | 10-20% | Minimum seat/usage threshold. Discount scales with commitment. |
| **Multi-year deal** | 15-25% | 2-year = 15%, 3-year = 20-25%. Locked in, not renegotiable year-over-year. |
| **Strategic logo** | 10-20% | Board-approved. Must include case study/reference rights. Document why this logo matters. |
| **Champion referral** | 5-10% | Prospect was referred by an existing customer. Reward the network. |
| **Annual prepay** | 5-10% | Paying the full year upfront vs. monthly billing. Cash flow benefit to you. |

### When NOT to Discount

| Scenario | Why Not | What to Do Instead |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| "It's too expensive" (no specifics) | They have not articulated what "too expensive" means. Discounting validates the objection without understanding it. | Ask: "Help me understand -- too expensive compared to what? Your budget? Another solution? The expected ROI?" |
| First proposal | Discounting before negotiation signals your price was inflated. | Hold. If they negotiate, you have room. If they accept, you protected margin. |
| "We need to think about it" | This is not a pricing objection. It is an unresolved concern. | Ask: "What specifically do you need to think about? Is it the investment, the scope, or something else?" |
| Small deal (< $10K) | Discount erodes margin on deals where margin is already thin. | Offer payment terms or reduced scope instead. |
| Repeat customer asking for "loyalty discount" | Creates expectation of perpetual discounts. | Offer value-adds: extra users, extended support, feature access. |

### The 10% Rule

Never discount more than 10% without getting something in return. Discounts are trades, not gifts.

| You Give | You Get |
|----------|---------|
| 10% discount | Multi-year commitment |
| 15% discount | Annual prepay + case study rights |
| 20% discount | 3-year deal + reference + expanded scope |
| Free pilot | Defined success criteria + decision date |

### How to Present Discounts

Never call it a "discount." Discounts signal that your original price was wrong.

**Instead, use:**
- "Annual commitment pricing" (not "annual discount")
- "Volume rate" (not "volume discount")
- "Partnership pricing" (not "special deal")
- "Early adopter rate" (for new products/tiers)

## Competitive Pricing Positioning

### When You Are More Expensive

This is the better position. Being more expensive signals premium quality -- but only if you justify it.

**Strategy: Reframe the comparison.**

1. **Expand the value equation.** "Their tool costs $30K. Ours costs $48K. But their implementation takes 6 months and ours takes 6 weeks. At $15K/month in lost productivity during implementation, their true cost is $120K."

2. **Compare total cost of ownership.** Include implementation time, training costs, integration costs, ongoing admin, and support quality. The cheapest tool is rarely the cheapest solution.

3. **Highlight risk.** "Saving $18K upfront but risking a failed implementation costs significantly more. Our 97% success rate means you get value faster."

4. **Use the "per-outcome" frame.** "$48K to generate $600K in pipeline is $0.08 per dollar of pipeline. Their tool at $30K generating $200K in pipeline costs $0.15 per dollar."

### When You Are Cheaper

Being cheaper is a disadvantage in enterprise sales -- it triggers "too good to be true" suspicion.

**Strategy: Explain why you are cheaper without being cheaper.**

1. **Efficiency story.** "We built our platform on modern infrastructure that costs us less to operate. We pass those savings to customers."

2. **Focus story.** "We do one thing extremely well. Competitors bundle features you will not use and charge for them."

3. **Growth story.** "We are investing in market share. This pricing reflects a partnership -- as you grow with us, we grow together."

**Never say:** "We're the affordable option." This is the kiss of death in B2B.

### Competitive Response Matrix

| Their Move | Your Response |
|-----------|--------------|
| "Competitor X is 40% cheaper" | "What does that include? Let me map our scope against theirs line by line." |
| "Competitor X offered us a discount" | "They may need to discount to close. Our pricing reflects the value we consistently deliver. Here are 3 references." |
| "We're leaning toward Competitor X" | "What specifically makes them the better fit? I want to make sure you're comparing the right things." |
| "Can you match their price?" | "I can not match a price without matching a scope. If we reduce to their price, here is what changes." |

## Payment Terms by Deal Type

### Standard Options

| Deal Size | Recommended Terms | Why |
|-----------|------------------|-----|
| < $10K | Monthly or quarterly billing | Low commitment, easy approval |
| $10K - $50K | Annual billing with monthly payment option | Encourages commitment, flexible on cash flow |
| $50K - $250K | Annual or multi-year, 50/50 split (signing + go-live) | Standard enterprise terms |
| $250K+ | Milestone-based (signing / implementation / go-live / quarterly) | Aligns payment with value delivery |

### By Company Type

| Company Type | Best Payment Structure | Notes |
|-------------|----------------------|-------|
| Startup (< 50 people) | Monthly billing | Cash flow sensitive. Make it easy to start. |
| Scale-up (50-500) | Annual with quarterly option | Growing budget, can commit but need flexibility |
| Enterprise (500+) | Annual or multi-year | Procurement prefers annual POs. Offer multi-year for discount. |
| Agency/Consultancy | Per-project or retainer | Revenue is project-based. Align with their model. |
| Non-profit / Education | Annual with extended net terms (Net-60) | Budget cycles are rigid. Be flexible on timing. |

### Payment Term Negotiation Levers

When a prospect asks for better terms, use these trades:

| They Want | You Offer | You Get |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Net-90 instead of Net-30 | Net-60 as compromise | Signed by end of quarter |
| Monthly billing | Monthly at 10% premium vs. annual | Flexibility at a fair price |
| Deferred start (pay later) | 30-day deferred billing | Case study commitment |
| Reduced upfront | 25/25/25/25 quarterly split | Multi-year term |

## Common Pricing Objections & Response Frameworks

Use the ACE framework for every pricing objection: **Acknowledge, Clarify, Explore.**

### Objection 1: "It's too expensive"

**Acknowledge:** "I hear you, and I want to make sure the investment makes sense for your team."

**Clarify:** "When you say too expensive -- compared to your budget, compared to another solution you're evaluating, or compared to what you expected?"

**Explore (based on answer):**
- *Budget*: "What budget range are you working with? There may be a way to phase the rollout."
- *Competitor*: "Let me compare scope side by side. Price without context can be misleading."
- *Expectations*: "Walk me through what you were expecting. I want to understand the gap."

### Objection 2: "We need to see ROI first"

**Acknowledge:** "Absolutely. You should expect a clear return."

**Clarify:** "What metrics would prove ROI for your team? Pipeline generated? Time saved? Revenue closed?"

**Explore:** "Based on what {contact} shared about {pain point costing $X/month}, the Growth tier pays for itself in {Y weeks}. Would a 60-day pilot with defined success metrics help you validate that?"

### Objection 3: "Can you do better on price?"

**Acknowledge:** "I appreciate you asking directly."

**Clarify:** "Is this about the overall investment, or is there a specific line item that feels out of line?"

**Explore:** "I can explore options. If we do annual prepay, I can bring the monthly effective rate down 10%. Or if you commit to a 2-year term, we can offer partnership pricing. What works better for your budget cycle?"

**Rule:** Never say "yes" immediately. Always trade.

### Objection 4: "We don't have budget right now"

**Acknowledge:** "Budget timing is real. Let me ask a couple questions so we can plan."

**Clarify:** "Is this a 'no budget this quarter' or 'no budget at all'? When does your fiscal year start?"

**Explore (if timing):** "Would it help to start with a smaller scope now and expand in Q{next}? We can also structure a deferred start where billing begins {date}."

**Explore (if real):** "I understand. Let me put together a business case document that {contact} can use to request budget. We have seen {similar company} use this approach to get approval in {timeframe}."

### Objection 5: "Competitor X is cheaper"

**Acknowledge:** "I would be surprised if they were not. Let me explain why."

**Clarify:** "Are they quoting the same scope? What is included in their price?"

**Explore:** "Let me build a total-cost comparison. In our experience, {competitor} requires {additional cost 1}, {additional cost 2}, and {additional time for implementation}. When you factor those in, the real difference is often smaller than it appears -- and sometimes reversed."

### Objection 6: "We need to involve procurement"

**Acknowledge:** "Of course. We have worked with procurement teams at companies like {reference customer}."

**Clarify:** "What does the procurement process look like? How long does it typically take? Is there anything I can prepare in advance to speed things up?"

**Explore:** "I will send over our standard security documentation, vendor questionnaire, and a procurement-ready summary. Typically that saves 2-3 weeks in the process. Who should I send it to?"

**Rule:** Procurement involvement is not an objection -- it is a buying signal. They would not send it to procurement if they did not intend to buy. Help, do not panic.
