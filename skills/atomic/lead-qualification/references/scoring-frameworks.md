## Framework Overview

Every qualification framework is a lens for the same underlying question: "Is this lead worth our time?" The difference is what each lens prioritizes and how deeply it probes.

| Framework | Primary Focus | Best For | Complexity | Time to Apply |
|-----------|--------------|----------|------------|---------------|
| BANT | Can they buy? | SMB/mid-market, transactional | Low | 2-5 minutes |
| MEDDICC | Will they buy from us? | Enterprise, complex deals | High | 15-30 minutes |
| 5-Dimension | Rapid first-pass scoring | Any, especially data-sparse | Low-Medium | 3-7 minutes |

## BANT: Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline

### Origin and Philosophy
IBM created BANT in the 1960s as a simple filter for mainframe sales. Its elegance is its simplicity: four questions that, when all answered affirmatively, indicate a qualified lead. Sixty years later, it remains the most widely recognized framework.

### Criteria Definitions

| Criterion | What You Are Assessing | Key Questions |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Budget** | Does the prospect have allocated funds (or the ability to allocate) for a purchase in your price range? | "What budget have you set aside for this?" / "How does your org typically fund new tool purchases?" |
| **Authority** | Is this person the final decision-maker, or can they directly influence the decision? | "Who else is involved in evaluating solutions like this?" / "Walk me through your typical purchase process." |
| **Need** | Does the prospect have a genuine problem that your product solves? | "What prompted you to look into this?" / "What happens if this problem goes unsolved for another 6 months?" |
| **Timeline** | Is there a defined window for making a decision and implementing a solution? | "When are you looking to have a solution in place?" / "Is there a specific event driving the timeline?" |

### BANT Scoring Rubric

| Score | Budget | Authority | Need | Timeline |
|-------|--------|-----------|------|----------|
| **5** | Explicit budget confirmed, within your price range | Final decision-maker with signing authority | Critical, urgent problem you solve directly | Decision within 30 days, implementation timeline set |
| **4** | Budget likely available, category spend confirmed | Strong influencer who owns the recommendation | Clear problem, actively seeking solutions | Decision within 60 days, general timeline |
| **3** | Budget possible, no explicit confirmation | Mid-level with input but not final say | Recognized problem but not yet prioritized | Decision within 90 days, no specific urgency |
| **2** | Budget constrained or unconfirmed | IC or early evaluator with limited influence | Vague awareness, no active search | No timeline, "maybe next year" |
| **1** | Known constraints or misalignment | No buying authority, wrong department | No clear need, or need does not match product | No timeline, purely exploratory |

### BANT Worked Example 1: Mid-Market SaaS Lead

**Prospect**: Sarah Chen, Director of Marketing at a 150-person SaaS company. Came in through a webinar registration. Company raised Series B 8 months ago.

- **Budget**: Series B funding suggests budget capacity. Director-level typically has $50K-100K discretionary spend. No explicit budget mention. **Score: 3**
- **Authority**: Director of Marketing at 150-person company -- likely owns tool decisions for her team, but VP or CMO probably signs off on larger purchases. **Score: 4**
- **Need**: Registered for a product-category webinar, implying awareness. But webinar attendance is low-intent. **Score: 3**
- **Timeline**: No timeline signals. Webinar attendees are often early-stage. **Score: 2**
- **BANT Average**: 3.0 -- Warm, needs discovery to confirm budget and timeline.

### BANT Worked Example 2: Enterprise Inbound

**Prospect**: James Park, VP of Revenue Operations at a 2,000-person fintech. Submitted a demo request form with the note: "Evaluating tools for our Q2 tech stack refresh. Budget approved, need a recommendation by March 1."

- **Budget**: "Budget approved" -- explicit confirmation. **Score: 5**
- **Authority**: VP of RevOps at 2,000-person company -- typically owns tool evaluation and recommends to CRO. Strong authority but may not sign. **Score: 4**
- **Need**: Active evaluation, explicitly stated they need a tool in this category. **Score: 5**
- **Timeline**: "Recommendation by March 1" -- concrete deadline, Q2 implementation. **Score: 5**
- **BANT Average**: 4.75 -- Hot. Fast-track immediately.

### BANT Worked Example 3: Weak Outbound

**Prospect**: Lisa Wong, Marketing Coordinator at FreshBites (12-person food delivery startup). Cold outbound, no response to initial email, opened second email but did not click.

- **Budget**: 12-person startup, no known funding. Marketing Coordinator has zero discretionary budget. **Score: 1**
- **Authority**: Coordinator = IC with no buying authority. **Score: 1**
- **Need**: No stated need. Opened an email but that is minimal signal. **Score: 1**
- **Timeline**: Cold outbound, no engagement. **Score: 1**
- **BANT Average**: 1.0 -- Disqualified. Too small, wrong person, no intent.

### When to Use BANT
- Deal sizes under $25K ACV
- Sales cycles under 30 days
- High-volume qualification (20+ leads per day)
- As a preliminary filter before deeper qualification with MEDDICC

### BANT Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths**: Simple, fast, universal vocabulary. Anyone can learn it in 5 minutes.

**Weaknesses**: Surface-level. Budget-first ordering is outdated (modern buyers discover need before securing budget). Does not account for competitive dynamics, buying process complexity, or champion identification.

## MEDDICC: The Enterprise Standard

### Origin and Philosophy
Jack Napoli and Dick Dunkel developed MEDDIC at PTC in the 1990s, growing the company from $300M to $1B in revenue. The second "C" (Competition) was added later. MEDDICC treats each deal as a multi-stakeholder puzzle and systematically maps the political and procedural landscape.

### Criteria Definitions

| Criterion | What You Are Assessing | Key Questions |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Metrics** | What quantifiable outcomes will the prospect use to measure success? | "What KPIs will you use to evaluate ROI?" / "What does success look like 6 months after deployment?" |
| **Economic Buyer** | Who has the final veto power and budget authority? | "Who signs the PO?" / "Who could kill this deal even if everyone else is on board?" |
| **Decision Criteria** | What specific requirements will drive the final decision? | "What are your must-haves vs. nice-to-haves?" / "How will you score vendors?" |
| **Decision Process** | What steps, approvals, and timelines will the org follow? | "Walk me through your evaluation process from here to signed contract." |
| **Identify Pain** | What specific, quantifiable pain is driving the evaluation? | "What is this problem costing you today?" / "What triggered this search?" |
| **Champion** | Who inside the org will actively advocate for your solution? | "Who on your team is most affected by this problem?" |
| **Competition** | Who else is being evaluated, and how do you compare? | "Who else are you looking at?" / "What criteria are you using to compare?" |

### MEDDICC Scoring Rubric

| Score | What It Means |
|-------|--------------|
| **5** | Criterion fully validated with evidence. Metrics quantified. Champion confirmed and active. Economic buyer identified and engaged. |
| **4** | Largely validated. Strong signals but not fully confirmed. Champion identified but not yet fully engaged. |
| **3** | Partial information. Some signals but significant gaps. Champion suspected but not confirmed. |
| **2** | Minimal information. Assumptions outweigh evidence. No clear champion yet. |
| **1** | No information or negative signals. Cannot identify key stakeholders. |

### MEDDICC Worked Example 1: Strong Enterprise Deal

**Prospect**: Acme Corp, 1,500 employees, Series D fintech. Your champion is David Liu, Director of Sales Enablement. Economic buyer is the CRO, Maria Santos. Acme is evaluating you and two competitors.

- **Metrics**: David shared KPIs: "Reduce rep ramp time from 45 to 20 days, increase quota attainment from 62% to 75%." Quantified and specific. **Score: 5**
- **Economic Buyer**: CRO Maria Santos identified. David confirms she has final sign-off. She attended the second demo and asked ROI questions. **Score: 4**
- **Decision Criteria**: David shared the internal scorecard: integration with Salesforce (must-have), AI-powered coaching (must-have), mobile app (nice-to-have), SOC2 compliance (must-have). **Score: 5**
- **Decision Process**: "We'll do 3 demos (done), then a pilot with 10 reps (starting next week), then Maria presents to the exec team. Final decision by April 15." **Score: 5**
- **Identify Pain**: "We lost 3 enterprise deals last quarter because reps couldn't articulate our value prop. CEO called it out in the all-hands. This is the #1 priority for our team." **Score: 5**
- **Champion**: David is actively coordinating evaluations, shared internal docs, and asked "What do you need from me to make this happen?" **Score: 5**
- **Competition**: Evaluating Gong and Chorus alongside you. David says "Gong is the safe choice but we think you're more innovative." **Score: 4**

**MEDDICC Average**: 4.7 -- Exceptionally strong deal. Focus on competitive positioning against Gong.

### MEDDICC Worked Example 2: Weak Enterprise Deal

**Prospect**: BigRetail Corp, 8,000 employees. Your contact is Tom Wright, Senior Analyst in the procurement department. Attended a conference booth demo.

- **Metrics**: Tom says "We want to improve sales productivity" but cannot quantify current state or target. **Score: 2**
- **Economic Buyer**: Unknown. Tom reports to a Procurement Manager. No access to sales leadership. **Score: 1**
- **Decision Criteria**: "We're just exploring what's out there." No scorecard, no requirements doc. **Score: 1**
- **Decision Process**: "I don't know yet. I'd need to talk to my manager." **Score: 1**
- **Identify Pain**: Vague. "Things could be better" but no specific pain, no quantified cost, no triggering event. **Score: 2**
- **Champion**: Tom is not a champion -- he is an evaluator with no influence. **Score: 1**
- **Competition**: "I'm looking at a few things" but won't share names. **Score: 2**

**MEDDICC Average**: 1.4 -- Do not invest. Wrong contact, no pain, no process. If you pursue this, find a champion in the sales org first.

### MEDDICC Performance Data
- MEDDICC-trained teams see 12-15% higher win rates on average (Gong, 2024, 2.5M deals)
- 20% shorter sales cycles for enterprise deals by identifying blockers earlier
- 35% more accurate forecasts compared to BANT-only (Forrester, 2023)
- Deals with 5+ MEDDICC criteria validated close at 3x the rate of those with 2 or fewer

### When to Use MEDDICC
- Enterprise deals above $50K ACV
- Sales cycles longer than 90 days
- Multi-stakeholder buying committees (3+ decision influencers)
- As a deepening framework AFTER initial qualification confirms the lead is worth pursuing
- Competitive deals where positioning matters

## The 5-Dimension Scoring Model

### Philosophy and Design
The default framework for this skill. Designed for rapid first-pass qualification where data is often incomplete. Unlike BANT or MEDDICC, the 5-Dimension model can be applied entirely from available data (CRM, web, enrichment APIs) before any conversation occurs.

### Why These Five Dimensions

| Dimension | Weight | Why This Weight |
|-----------|--------|----------------|
| Company Size Fit | 25% | Strongest single predictor. Controls budget capacity, complexity requirements, and cycle length. |
| Industry Fit | 25% | Determines value proposition resonance and proof point availability. |
| Role Seniority & Authority | 20% | Controls decision-making ability. Slightly lower because authority can be navigated (find the right person). |
| Budget Signals | 15% | Important but usually indirect at first-pass. Weight reflects data availability. |
| Timing & Intent | 15% | Critical differentiator between "good fit" and "good fit right now." Most volatile dimension. |

### 5-Dimension vs Other Frameworks

| Dimension | BANT Equivalent | MEDDICC Equivalent | What 5-Dimension Adds |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Company Size Fit | (not explicit) | (not explicit) | Explicit ICP matching against firmographic data |
| Industry Fit | (not explicit) | (not explicit) | Proof point availability assessment |
| Role Authority | Authority | Economic Buyer + Champion | Title-to-company-size calibration |
| Budget Signals | Budget | Metrics (indirect) | Proxy signal analysis (funding, hiring, tech spend) |
| Timing & Intent | Timeline | Identify Pain + Decision Process | Behavioral signal weighting over demographic signals |

### Source Quality Multiplier

Unique to 5-Dimension. Applied because source data is always available and independently predictive:

| Source | Multiplier | Data Source |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Customer referral | 1.25x | 4x conversion rate vs cold inbound (Salesforce, 2024) |
| Partner referral | 1.15x | 2.5x conversion rate vs cold inbound |
| Demo request | 1.15x | Explicit high intent |
| Content inbound | 1.0x | Baseline |
| Event lead | 1.0x | Mixed signal, highly variable |
| Cold outbound | 0.9x | Lower initial intent |
| Purchased list | 0.8x | Lowest quality, highest data accuracy issues |

### 5-Dimension Worked Example 1: Strong Inbound (SaaS Mid-Market)

**Context**: Maria Torres, VP of Sales at CloudOps (280 employees, Series B, DevOps SaaS). Requested a demo after downloading a case study. Found via Google search for "[your product category] for DevOps teams."

| Dimension | Score | Weight | Weighted | Reasoning | Layer Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Company Size | 5 | 25% | 1.25 | 280 employees -- squarely in ICP sweet spot of 100-500 | L1: CRM, L2: Web (LinkedIn) |
| Industry Fit | 5 | 25% | 1.25 | DevOps SaaS -- target vertical with 3 existing case studies | L1: CRM, L3: RAG (similar wins) |
| Role Authority | 5 | 20% | 1.00 | VP of Sales at 280-person company = clear decision-maker | L2: Enrichment (title verified) |
| Budget Signals | 4 | 15% | 0.60 | Series B = strong budget signal, no explicit confirmation | L2: Web (Crunchbase funding) |
| Timing & Intent | 5 | 15% | 0.75 | Demo request + case study + active Google search = strong intent | L4: Behavioral |

**Raw Score**: 4.85 | **Source Multiplier**: Demo request = 1.15x | **Final Score**: 5.0 (capped)
**Tier**: HOT | **Confidence**: High

### 5-Dimension Worked Example 2: Data-Sparse Lead

**Context**: Alex Kim, unknown title, at TechVenture (unknown size). Email only: alex@techventure.io. Source: outbound cold list.

| Dimension | Score | Weight | Weighted | Reasoning | Layer Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Company Size | 0 | 25% | 0.00 | Cannot determine -- no data | None |
| Industry Fit | 0 | 25% | 0.00 | Cannot determine -- no data | None |
| Role Authority | 0 | 20% | 0.00 | Cannot determine -- no title | None |
| Budget Signals | 0 | 15% | 0.00 | Cannot determine -- no data | None |
| Timing & Intent | 1 | 15% | 0.15 | Cold list = lowest intent signal | L1: Source data |

**Raw Score**: 0.15 | **Source Multiplier**: Purchased list = 0.8x | **Final Score**: 0.12
**Tier**: DISQUALIFIED (pending enrichment) | **Confidence**: LOW
**Next Action**: Run `lead-research` then `sales-enrich` to populate Layers 1-2 before scoring.

### 5-Dimension Worked Example 3: Borderline Lead with Enrichment Value

**Context**: Rachel Green, "Head of Growth" at NovaTech (unknown size). Inbound via content download (whitepaper on "scaling outbound sales"). Email domain is novatech.io.

| Dimension | Score | Weight | Weighted | Reasoning | Layer Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Company Size | 0 | 25% | 0.00 | Cannot determine. Web search found LinkedIn page showing ~85 employees. | L2: Web |
| Industry Fit | 3 | 25% | 0.75 | Tech company (from domain). No case studies in exact vertical but adjacent. | L2: Web |
| Role Authority | 4 | 20% | 0.80 | "Head of Growth" at ~85-person company = likely reports to CEO/CRO. Strong influence. | L2: Enrichment |
| Budget Signals | 0 | 15% | 0.00 | No funding data found. Cannot assess. | None |
| Timing & Intent | 3 | 15% | 0.45 | Whitepaper download = awareness stage. Topic-relevant but not product-specific. | L4: Behavioral |

**Raw Score**: 2.00 | **Source Multiplier**: Content inbound = 1.0x | **Final Score**: 2.0
**Tier**: COLD (but borderline -- 1.0 point below Warm)
**Confidence**: LOW (3 of 5 dimensions partially scored, 2 at zero)
**Key Swing Factor**: If company size confirmed at 85+ AND funding discovered, score jumps to ~3.2 (Warm). Recommend enrichment.
**Next Action**: Chain to `lead-research` for company deep-dive. If NovaTech is 80+ employees with any funding, re-score and upgrade to Warm.

## Framework Selection Decision Tree

```
START: What is the estimated ACV?
  |
  |-- Under $10K ACV
  |     --> BANT or 5-Dimension (speed matters, qualify at volume)
  |
  |-- $10K-$50K ACV
  |     |
  |     |-- Sales cycle < 60 days?
  |     |     --> BANT for first pass, 5-Dimension for enriched scoring
  |     |
  |     |-- Sales cycle 60-120 days?
  |           --> 5-Dimension for first pass, BANT for discovery calls
  |
  |-- $50K-$200K ACV
  |     |
  |     |-- Single decision-maker?
  |     |     --> 5-Dimension for first pass, BANT for discovery
  |     |
  |     |-- Multi-stakeholder (3+)?
  |           --> 5-Dimension for first pass, MEDDICC for deal progression
  |
  |-- Over $200K ACV
        --> 5-Dimension for first pass, MEDDICC mandatory for deal progression
```

### Framework Stacking (Best Practice)

Top-performing teams stack frameworks across the deal lifecycle:

1. **First Pass (inbound arrival)**: 5-Dimension scoring to assign tier and priority. Uses all 5 intelligence layers.
2. **Discovery Call**: BANT (simple deals) or MEDDICC (complex deals) to validate assumptions
3. **Deal Progression**: MEDDICC (enterprise) to track deal health and identify blockers
4. **Competitive Deals**: MEDDICC's Competition criterion supplemented by the `competitor-intel` skill

## Score Recalibration Guide

Scoring weights are starting points. They must be calibrated to your actual win/loss data over time.

### When to Recalibrate

- **Monthly**: Review tier accuracy. Are leads scored Hot actually closing at Hot rates?
- **Quarterly**: Analyze dimension-level predictive power. Which dimensions best predicted actual outcomes?
- **After major product/market changes**: New pricing, new vertical, new product -- scoring assumptions change.

### How to Recalibrate: Step-by-Step

#### Step 1: Pull the Data
For the last 90 days, export:
- All qualified leads with their original 5-Dimension scores (per-dimension and total)
- Outcome: converted to opportunity? Closed-won? Closed-lost? Still open?
- Actual sales cycle length
- Actual deal value

#### Step 2: Calculate Per-Dimension Correlation
For each dimension, calculate the correlation between the dimension score and the outcome (1 = closed-won, 0 = not):

| Dimension | Original Weight | Correlation with Close | Suggested Weight |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Company Size | 25% | 0.42 | 28% |
| Industry Fit | 25% | 0.31 | 22% |
| Role Authority | 20% | 0.38 | 25% |
| Budget Signals | 15% | 0.22 | 13% |
| Timing & Intent | 15% | 0.35 | 12% |

In this example, Role Authority was more predictive than originally weighted, while Timing & Intent was less predictive. Adjust weights accordingly.

#### Step 3: Validate Tier Boundaries
Check if the tier boundaries (4.0, 3.0, 2.0) are still correct:

| Tier Boundary | Expected Win Rate | Actual Win Rate | Adjustment |
|--------------|-------------------|----------------|------------|
| Hot (>= 4.0) | 25-35% | 32% | No change needed |
| Warm (3.0-3.9) | 10-20% | 8% | Consider raising to 3.2 |
| Cold (2.0-2.9) | 3-8% | 5% | No change needed |

If Warm leads are winning at Cold rates, the Warm threshold is too low.

#### Step 4: Validate Source Multipliers
Check actual conversion by source:

| Source | Current Multiplier | Actual Conversion vs Baseline | Adjusted Multiplier |
|--------|-------------------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| Customer referral | 1.25x | 4.1x baseline | 1.25x (confirmed) |
| Partner referral | 1.15x | 1.8x baseline | 1.10x (lower than expected) |
| Demo request | 1.15x | 2.9x baseline | 1.20x (higher than expected) |

#### Step 5: Document and Deploy
Update the scoring framework with new weights and multipliers. Note the date, data window, and rationale for each change.

### Common Recalibration Mistakes

1. **Over-fitting to small samples**: Do not recalibrate based on fewer than 50 closed outcomes. Statistical noise will mislead you.
2. **Ignoring seasonality**: Q4 deals close differently than Q1. Use rolling 90-day windows, not single-month snapshots.
3. **Recalibrating too often**: Monthly reviews, quarterly adjustments. Changing weights every week creates chaos.
4. **Forgetting to recalibrate source multipliers**: Sources shift. A channel partner that sent great leads last year may send lower quality this year.

## Industry-Specific Scoring Adjustments

### SaaS / Technology

| Dimension | Adjustment | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Company Size | Weight normally (25%) | Standard predictor |
| Industry Fit | Increase to 30% if you have strong vertical case studies | Tech buyers heavily weight vendor domain expertise |
| Budget Signals | Funding stage is a strong proxy. Series B+ = +1 to budget score | Venture-backed companies have allocated tool budgets |
| Timing & Intent | Product-led signals (free trial signup, API docs visit) are very high intent | Self-serve behavior predicts conversion strongly |

### Professional Services (Legal, Consulting, Accounting)

| Dimension | Adjustment | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Company Size | Use headcount AND partner count (partner count is more predictive for firms) | Partner-led firms make decisions differently |
| Role Authority | Partner/Managing Director = 5. Senior Associate = 2 max. Title hierarchy is strict. | Authority is extremely concentrated in professional services |
| Budget Signals | Billable rate x headcount is a better budget proxy than funding | Professional services firms are rarely venture-funded |
| Industry Fit | Reduce to 20%. Professional services firms buy tools that match their workflow, not their clients' industry. | Cross-industry applicability matters more |

### Marketplace / E-commerce

| Dimension | Adjustment | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Company Size | Use GMV (gross merchandise value) instead of headcount | A 30-person marketplace doing $50M GMV has very different buying capacity than a 30-person SaaS company |
| Budget Signals | Increase weight to 20%. Transaction volume and margins are visible proxies. | Marketplace unit economics are more transparent |
| Timing & Intent | Seasonal adjustment. Q4 = peak for e-commerce. Leads in October are more urgent. | Buying decisions cluster around peak seasons |

### Hardware / Manufacturing

| Dimension | Adjustment | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Company Size | Use revenue rather than headcount. Manufacturing is capital-intensive with fewer employees per dollar. | A 200-person manufacturer can have $500M revenue |
| Industry Fit | Critical. Manufacturing sub-verticals (automotive, aerospace, consumer goods) have very different needs. Increase to 30%. | Cross-vertical assumptions are dangerous |
| Budget Signals | Capex cycles matter. Check for recent facility expansion, new production lines, or equipment purchases. | Budget availability is cyclical, not continuous |
| Timing & Intent | Longer cycles are normal. A "3" in timing for manufacturing is equivalent to a "4" in SaaS. | 6-12 month evaluation cycles are standard |

### Consulting / Agency

| Dimension | Adjustment | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Company Size | Lower minimums. A 15-person consultancy is mid-market in agency land. Adjust ICP ranges down. | Agencies are structurally smaller but can have significant budgets |
| Role Authority | Founder/Partner = 5 even at a 10-person firm. Agencies are founder-driven. | Flat hierarchies, concentrated authority |
| Budget Signals | Project-based budgets, not annual. Ask "What is this project's budget?" not "What is your annual tool spend?" | Agencies budget by engagement, not annually |
| Timing & Intent | Client-driven urgency. "We just won a big account and need to scale" is a 5. | Agencies buy when they win clients |
